Frogfish

Anglerfish

They don't resemble frogs, but they hop around a lot.

Basic Information

  • Family: Antennariidae
  • Number of species: ~54
  • Size: 3-38cm
  • Range: Pretty much in all tropical oceans.

Frogfish live on the ocean floor and around coral and rock reefs (100m deep), and are ambush predators, as they use their lures to reel in their prey, and catch them. But the way they do this is nothing short of fascinating.

Movement

Frogfish don't swim. At all. They can't, they don't have the organ necessary to have buoyancy unlike most other fish, which is just called the "swim bladder." This doesn't leave the frogfish as completely immobile though, as they have very weird fins.

A frogfish's pectoral fins have developed joints, which allow the frogfish to "walk" across the seafloor and other places. This is their main form of movement, and it's very slow, but it works out as their ambush predators, they don't chase their food around.

If, however, a frogfish wants to pick up the pace, they have a very special way to do it. Their gills (which are positioned on the "elbows" of their pectoral fins) can also function as mini jetpacks! That's right, by gulping in water and then pushing it out of the gill openings, a frogfish can propel themselves in short but strong bursts. It's like strapping on 2 leafblowers to avoid swimming. It's really cool to see in person.

However, there is one species of frogfish that is able to swim. The Sargassum Frogfish DOES have a swim bladder, and also have the interesting feature of their fins being built to be able to climb on top of sargassum seaweed.

Camoflauge + Lures

Frogfish being ambus predators has been mentioned a couple times now, but are they good at being stealthy? Indeed they are, they are the top class in their game.

Frogfish are both capable of changing colors, while also imitating any sort decoration their surroundings have. This takes a few weeks to be fully realized, but once the camoflauge it's complete, it could fool just about anything

Frogfish can mimic coral reefs, rocks, seaweed, and other enviroments. They can attach algae on themselves, grow hair like extensions, and can even mimic the insides of corals. One species even resemble sea urchins They take camoflauge to the next level.

Their lures are no different. Located at the tip of their first dorsal fin, a frogfish's lure (or illicum in a more scientific term) can be freakishly lifelike. Some can mimic worms, others debree, and the most advanced can even mimic shrimp. They have no predation patterns, even if their lures seem to be highly specific. Alongside looking like the real deal, they can move their lures just as lifelike, with recently discovered motor neurons allowing them to do so. If their lures were ever to get detached in any way, they can simply just regenerate it in a couple weeks. Combined, the frogfish makes for a deadly predator to it's diet.

Fastest Bite in the Sea + It's Diet

Ok, the frogfish has lured in a tiny cod by wiggling around it's wormlike lure, how would the frogfish make it's meal? By using it's bite of 6 miliseconds of course. By quickly opening it's jaws (which it can expand 12 times its original size!), a frogfish quickly gulps its prey, whole, and once it gets into its stomach, uses a special muscle to trap them.

A Frogfish's bite is so fast that you need to slow down the footage over 40 times to have a slight idea on what's going on.

A frogfish's diet are small crustaceans, other fish (they can eat fish that match their size!), and even themselves. Frogfish are cannibals when they want to be. Horrific.
